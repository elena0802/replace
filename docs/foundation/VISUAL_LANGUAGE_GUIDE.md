# Re:Place Visual Language Guide

**Document type:** Brand & visual identity handbook  
**Companion to:** Re:Place Design System v2 (tokens and components)  
**Audience:** Designers, engineers, contributors, AI agents  
**Version:** 1.0

---

> Re:Place is a quiet lifestyle archive for meaningful places and memories.  
> It is not a feed. It is not a dashboard. It is a book you reopen.

This guide defines *how Re:Place should feel* — emotionally, visually, and narratively. When Design System v2 tells you *what* to build, this document tells you *why* and *whether it belongs*.

---

# 1. Brand Personality

## Keywords

**Calm · Warm · Intentional · Timeless · Trustworthy · Reflective · Curated · Human**

## What Re:Place Is

Re:Place is a personal archive for places that mattered — cafés where you slowed down, parks where you walked alone, hotels where a trip became a memory. It honors the act of *remembering*, not the performance of sharing.

The product behaves like a place journal someone keeps for themselves first, and offers to others only when ready. Every surface should respect slowness: room to read, room to pause, room to return.

Re:Place believes that a single honest sentence about an afternoon can be more valuable than a rating, a ranking, or a reaction count.

## What Re:Place Is NOT

| Re:Place is not… | Because… |
|------------------|------------|
| A social media app | Social products optimize for engagement loops, virality, and comparison. Re:Place optimizes for recall and care. |
| A productivity dashboard | Dashboards compress life into metrics. Re:Place expands a moment into memory. |
| A startup SaaS | SaaS UI shouts features, tiers, and urgency. Re:Place whispers invitation. |
| A review platform | Stars and scores flatten experience. Re:Place preserves nuance. |
| A travel booking tool | Booking is transactional. Re:Place is archival. |
| A discovery feed | Infinite scroll encourages consumption. Re:Place encourages return visits to what you already love. |
| A gamified habit tracker | Streaks and badges create anxiety. Re:Place creates attachment. |

# 2. Emotional Goals

## Opening the app

Users should feel **welcomed, unhurried, and oriented** — as if opening a notebook on a quiet morning. No pressure to act. No fear of falling behind. The first emotion is *ease*, not *obligation*.

> “I can take my time here.”

## Recording a place

Users should feel **present and expressive** — not evaluated. Recording is an act of care: choosing words, optionally adding a photo, marking how much they want to return. The interface should feel like writing, not submitting a form to an algorithm.

> “This moment deserves to be kept.”

## Revisiting old places

Users should feel **nostalgia without sadness, clarity without clutter** — memories resurface gently. Old records should read like entries written by a past self, not stale database rows. Layout and typography should make revisiting pleasurable, not archaeological.

> “I’m glad I wrote this down.”

## After finishing a record

Users should feel **quiet satisfaction and closure** — a sense of something placed carefully on a shelf. Not dopamine from a badge or notification. A soft confirmation, then return to the archive or the next moment of life.

> “That’s saved. I can come back anytime.”

# 3. Visual Principles

## Quiet over loud

Visual noise competes with memory. Re:Place uses restraint: soft contrast, muted accents, few competing focal points. One hero moment per screen. Buttons speak; they don’t shout. Color appears where meaning lives — category, state, action — not as decoration.

*If everything is emphasized, nothing is remembered.*

## Editorial over decorative

Layout follows reading rhythm: headline, lead, body, aside. Typography carries hierarchy. Ornament is rare and purposeful — a thin rule, a gentle tag, a well-placed image. Avoid stickers, confetti, gratuitous gradients, and “delight” that distracts from content.

*The place and the memory are the artwork.*

## Spacious over dense

White space is not waste; it is breathing room for reflection. Sections are separated clearly. Cards do not crowd each other. Forms unfold progressively rather than dumping every field at once. Density signals urgency; Re:Place rejects urgency.

*Space signals respect for the user’s attention.*

## Human over technical

Interfaces should feel written by a person who loves places, not configured by a platform. Copy is warm and specific. Errors are apologetic and helpful, never diagnostic. Empty states invite rather than instruct. Metadata (dates, regions, tags) supports story — it does not replace it.

*Users should never feel they are using infrastructure.*

## Timeless over trendy

Avoid visual trends with short half-lives: glass blur, neon accents, hyper-rounded “friendly SaaS” blobs, dark-mode-purple gradients, 3D icons. Re:Place should look as appropriate in five years as today — like a well-designed book, not a dated app screenshot.

*Trends date memories. Timelessness preserves them.*

## Trustworthy over impressive

Premium does not mean flashy. It means consistent, honest, and complete. Real content over mock data. Clear ownership of public vs private. No dark patterns. No fake social proof. Visual polish serves credibility, not spectacle.

*Trust is built when the product disappears behind the memory.*

## Curated over chaotic

Every grid is an arrangement, not a dump. Order implies intention — by date, by feeling, by collection theme. Even “explore” should feel like browsing a small gallery, not scraping a firehose.

*Curation is care made visible.*

# 4. Photography Direction

Photography is the emotional anchor of Re:Place. Images should feel *remembered*, not *optimized for engagement*.

## Lighting

**Prefer:** Soft natural light — window light, overcast daylight, golden hour without harsh HDR. Gentle shadows that suggest time of day.

**Avoid:** Flash-heavy indoor shots, blown highlights, heavy filters, artificial “Instagram warmth” applied uniformly.

## Composition

**Prefer:** Thoughtful framing — a table by the window, a path through trees, a detail that carries atmosphere (cup, menu corner, chair, view). Off-center compositions welcome. Negative space allowed.

**Avoid:** Selfie-first framing, extreme wide-angle distortion, cluttered “everything in frame” tourist shots, stock-photo poses (laptop + latte + perfect notebook).

## Colors

**Prefer:** Colors true to the moment — muted earth tones, greens, wood, ceramic, fabric. Palette should harmonize with Re:Place’s sage/beige/olive UI without fighting it.

**Avoid:** Oversaturated edits, uniform orange-teal grading, neon signage as the dominant subject unless it’s the honest memory.

## Subjects

**Prefer:** The felt experience of being there — where you sat, what you saw, what made you stay. People optional and secondary; environment primary. Seasonal cues (light, foliage) welcome.

**Avoid:** Menu screenshots as hero images, receipt photos, parking lots, purely functional wayfinding unless no alternative exists.

## Mood

**Prefer:** Stillness, warmth, pause, intimacy, “I want to come back.” Photos should feel like turning a page, not posting an update.

**Avoid:** Performative luxury, aggressive “aesthetic” staging, party/club energy, shock or irony for attention.

## Photos that should NOT be used

- Generic Unsplash placeholders presented as real user memories in product surfaces
- Corporate stock photography with models smiling at salads
- Heavy text overlays, watermarks, or collage stickers
- Low-resolution screenshots from map apps as cover images
- Images that misrepresent the place or the user’s experience

# 5. Illustration & Empty States

## Should illustrations exist?

**Sparingly, and almost never as decoration.**

Re:Place is photo- and word-led. Illustration should appear only when photography cannot — empty collection covers, missing image placeholders, or rare onboarding moments. If illustration appears, it must feel **paper-like, minimal, and quiet** — single-color line or soft flat shapes in sage/olive/beige. No characters, no mascots, no cartoon maps.

## Why not more illustration?

Illustration shifts the product toward “app brand” and away from “personal archive.” Users’ photos and words are the visual identity. The system should get out of the way.

## How empty states should feel

Empty states are **invitations, not failures**.

They should feel like an unfilled page in a beautiful notebook — not an error, not a dead end. Tone: gentle, forward-looking, one clear next step. Visual treatment: generous padding, soft surface, optional simple icon or typographic emphasis — never a large whimsical illustration that steals focus.

> Good empty state energy: “아직 담긴 장소가 없어요. 첫 번째 기억을 남겨보세요.”  
> Bad empty state energy: “Oops! Nothing here yet!!! 🎉”

# 6. Iconography

Icons are functional signposts, not personality vehicles.

## Stroke width

**1.75px** at 24px canvas — consistent with location/copy icons already in the product. Never mix 1px hairlines with 2.5px bold strokes.

## Corner style

**Rounded caps and joins** — soft but not bubbly. Icons should feel drawn with a fine pen, not inflated for “friendliness.”

## Filled vs outline

**Outline by default.** Filled variants only for active/selected states (e.g., saved/bookmarked). Never mix filled and outline icons in the same toolbar.

## Size system

| Context | Size |
|---------|------|
| Inline with Small text | 16px |
| Buttons, list rows | 20px |
| Standalone actions | 24px |
| Empty state accent | 32px max |

## Visual consistency

- One icon family across the product — no Unicode symbols (hearts, arrows) standing in for icons
- Icons inherit text color (`secondary` default, `brand` on active)
- No multicolor icons except third-party brand marks (Kakao, Naver where required)
- Icons always paired with text labels on primary actions — icon-only reserved for universally understood actions (close, copy)

# 7. Layout Philosophy

## White space

White space creates **psychological quiet**. Page margins are generous. Content never touches viewport edges on mobile. Sections breathe before new topics begin. When choosing between “show more above the fold” and “let this entry rest,” choose rest.

## Reading rhythm

Follow **eyebrow → title → lead → content → action**:

1. Orient (where am I?)
2. Name (what is this?)
3. Context (why does it matter?)
4. substance (the memory, the places)
5. Optional action (one primary step)

Avoid stacking multiple headlines of equal weight. One story per screen section.

## Content hierarchy

**Memory leads. Metadata follows.** On a place detail page, the name and “한 줄 기록” dominate; category, region, and dates support. On cards, title + memory beat badges and chips. UI chrome (nav, buttons) should recede until needed.

## Section spacing

Sections are **chapters**, not dividers. Space between sections should be noticeably larger than space within sections. A new section signals a new thought — “위치,” “머물렀던 순간,” “공간 정보” — not a form field group.

## Card spacing

Cards in grids relate to each other like **entries on a shelf** — even gaps, aligned baselines, consistent image proportions. Never pack cards to maximize count. On mobile, one column with full-width cards often feels more archival than two cramped columns.

## Mobile-first thinking

Most meaningful recording happens **on location, on a phone**. Design for one-handed use, readable 16px body text, thumb-reachable primary actions, and short scroll paths. Desktop adds horizon and side-by-side reading — it does not introduce dashboard density.

# 8. Motion Philosophy

Motion in Re:Place is **atmosphere, not entertainment**.

## What should move

- **Feedback:** Button press acknowledgment, save confirmation, subtle hover lift on cards
- **Context changes:** Sheets and modals arriving softly from below or fading in — mimicking turning a page or opening a note
- **Loading:** Gentle skeleton pulse — content shape appearing before words
- **State:** Bookmark/save icon filling quietly when toggled

Motion should answer: *“Did my action land?”* or *“Where am I now?”*

## What should never move

- Continuous background animation
- Parallax scroll theatrics
- Bouncing buttons or elastic overshoot
- Auto-playing carousels
- Celebration confetti, streak flames, notification slides that demand attention
- Layout-shifting text blocks during load

## Desired feeling

Motion should feel like **closing a book cover** — deliberate, soft, final. Never like a slot machine or a timeline refresh.

## Examples

| Moment | Right feeling | Wrong feeling |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| Saving a place | Soft confirmation, stillness | Fireworks, badge pop |
| Opening a collection | Calm reveal | Rapid slide deck |
| Deleting a record | Slow modal, clear consequence | Shake animation, red flash |
| Loading a list | Skeleton placeholders | Spinning loader center stage |

Respect **reduced motion**: calmness must not require animation.

# 9. Writing Tone

Re:Place writes like a **thoughtful friend who keeps a beautiful notebook** — not a system administrator, not a growth marketer.

## Voice attributes

- **Warm** — human, never cold or corporate
- **Calm** — no exclamation marks unless truly warranted
- **Specific** — concrete nouns (장소, 순간, 기록) over abstractions (콘텐츠, 데이터)
- **Inviting** — suggest, don’t command
- **Honest** — especially in empty and error states

## Titles

Short, evocative, archival. Name the activity or collection — not the feature.

| Good | Avoid |
|------|-------|
| 나만의 장소 아카이브 | 대시보드 |
| 다시 가고 싶은 곳의 기록 | 피드 |
| 장소를 기억의 결로 묶기 | 컬렉션 관리 |

## Descriptions

One or two sentences. Sensory and purposeful. Explain *why* this page exists emotionally.

> “사람들이 남긴 좋은 장소와 머물렀던 순간을 차분히 둘러보세요.”

## Buttons

Verb-first, achievable, single outcome. No hype.

| Good | Avoid |
|------|-------|
| 장소 기록하기 | 지금 바로 시작!! |
| 저장하기 | Save |
| 둘러보기 | Explore Feed |
| 수정하기 | Edit Record v2 |

## Errors

Apologize briefly, explain simply, offer one recovery path. Never expose infrastructure.

| Good | Avoid |
|------|-------|
| 잠시 불러오지 못했어요. 다시 시도해주세요. | Supabase 설정을 확인해주세요 |
| 로그인이 필요해요 | AuthRequiredError |

## Empty states

Acknowledge absence without blame. Point forward.

> “아직 공개된 장소가 없어요. 첫 번째 기억을 남겨보세요.”

## Success messages

Understated closure — not celebration.

| Good | Avoid |
|------|-------|
| 좋은 장소가 기록되었습니다. | 🎉 기록 완료! 대단해요! |
| 컬렉션에서 장소를 제거했습니다. | Successfully deleted item |

## Avoid

- English UI copy mixed into Korean flows (except proper nouns)
- Technical jargon (CRUD, MVP, API, 설정, 에러 코드)
- Urgency language (지금 안 하면, 놓치지 마세요)
- Gamification (레벨 업, 연속 기록)
- SaaS tropes (Unlock Premium Power)

# 10. Image Cropping Rules

Consistency makes an archive feel **intentionally kept**, not accidentally uploaded.

## Preferred aspect ratios

| Context | Ratio | Rationale |
|---------|-------|-----------|
| Place card (grid) | **4:3** | Classic photo album feel; works portrait and landscape sources |
| Place detail hero | **16:10** | Cinematic but not ultra-wide; room for memory below |
| Collection cover | **16:10** | Matches place hero — collections feel like bound volumes |
| Featured / editorial | **4:3 or 16:10** | Pick one per section — never mix on same row |
| Thumbnails / avatars | **1:1** | Rare; use sparingly |

## Cover cropping behavior

- **Center-weighted crop** by default — assume subject is middle-third
- **Focal point priority:** if upload/crop UI allows, favor faces, table scenes, or architectural anchors — never crop off the sole meaningful subject
- **No aggressive zoom** that creates pixelated heroes

## Safe zones

- Keep **top 12% and bottom 12%** clear of critical subject matter — badges and text overlays may appear
- On collection covers, avoid placing key detail in **lower-right corner** (count badges often sit top-right; action menus may sit corners)

## Thumbnail consistency

All cards in a grid share **identical aspect ratio and corner radius**. Mixed heights in the same grid break the “shelf” illusion. Fallback empty tiles match live tile dimensions exactly — no shrinking placeholders.

## Authenticity

Crop to improve framing, not to misrepresent. A cozy corner should not be cropped to look like a vast landscape.

# 11. Accessibility Philosophy

Accessibility in Re:Place is not compliance bolted on — it **is** calm design made universal.

## Readable typography

- Body text stays at comfortable reading sizes — never shrink to fit more content
- Line height generous enough for Korean multi-line reading
- Hierarchy through size and weight, not color alone

*Clarity reduces cognitive load — calm for everyone.*

## Touch targets

Interactive elements are large enough to tap without precision stress — especially for users recording on location, walking, or one-handed. Small targets create friction; friction breaks calm.

## Contrast

Warm palettes must still meet readable contrast. Soft does not mean faint. Primary text on surfaces, button labels on fills, error text on blush backgrounds — all must remain legible in daylight and at night.

*Low contrast is not premium — it is exclusion.*

## Motion reduction

Users who disable motion should lose no information — only decoration. Calm is the default; animation is optional enhancement.

## Broader philosophy

An archive nobody can read is not trustworthy. Accessibility widens who gets to keep and revisit memories — aligned with Re:Place’s human-centered purpose.

# 12. Design Inspirations

Inspiration is **category and feeling**, not pixel-copying another product.

## Boutique hotel editorial

**Why it fits:** Hotels curate atmosphere — lobby, light, materials, pace. Re:Place curates memory with the same restraint. You feel cared for, not sold to.

*Takeaway:* Premium through material quality and space, not logos and loud CTAs.

## Independent magazine

**Why it fits:** Strong typographic hierarchy, photography with story, sections that breathe. You read cover to cover — or return to a favorite article.

*Takeaway:* Editorial layout; one feature per spread; captions matter.

## Museum guide

**Why it fits:** Labels are concise, respectful, contextual. Objects (places) are center stage; the system explains just enough.

*Takeaway:* Metadata supports artifact; no gamified tour rush.

## Japanese travel journal (旅ノート)

**Why it fits:** Personal, seasonal, detail-oriented — a stamp, a sketch, a sentence about rain. Quiet pride in small observations.

*Takeaway:* Honor the small moment; prefer nuance over comprehensiveness.

## Premium coffee book

**Why it fits:** Warm neutrals, texture, ritual, slowness. Photography of spaces where time stretches.

*Takeaway:* Warm palette, tactile surfaces, ritual of return (“다시 가고 싶은 마음”).

## What NOT to take from these inspirations

- Literal vintage paper textures that harm readability
- Overly austere minimalism that feels cold — Re:Place is warm, not sterile
- Exclusive luxury signaling — Re:Place is premium but approachable

# 13. Anti-Patterns

These directions **must never appear** in Re:Place. They conflict with calm, trust, and archival character.

| Anti-pattern | Why it conflicts |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Glassmorphism** | Trendy blur reads as SaaS / iOS novelty, not paper and memory |
| **Neon / electric accents** | Breaks warm olive-beige world; feels nightlife/social, not reflective |
| **Heavy gradients** | Decorative noise; dates quickly; competes with photography |
| **Loud animations** | Creates urgency and dopamine loops antithetical to journaling |
| **Dense dashboards** | Metrics and widgets imply monitoring, not remembering |
| **Notification-first layouts** | Red badges and pings demand attention — archive apps wait patiently |
| **Gamification** | Streaks, points, levels trivialize meaningful places |
| **Endless feeds** | Optimizes consumption volume over depth of recall |
| **Aggressive badges** | “NEW”, “HOT”, “99+” create FOMO — hostile to calm |
| **Clickbait cards** | Truncated hooks, reaction bait, mystery clicks — disrespect user intent |
| **Mock / fake content in marketing surfaces** | Destroys trust — the opposite of archival honesty |
| **Developer error copy** | Breaks human tone; exposes immaturity |
| **Comparison metrics** | Follower counts, popularity ranks — social hierarchy, not personal archive |
| **Dark patterns in monetization** | Urgency timers, hidden opt-outs — violates trust |
| **Uniform stock photography** | Makes every user’s archive look like the same brochure |
| **All-caps shouting CTAs** | Marketing aggression, not invitation |

# 14. The Re:Place Test

Before shipping any screen, flow, or feature, answer **yes** to every question that applies.

## Calm & character

- [ ] Does this feel **calm** at first glance — not busy, loud, or urgent?
- [ ] Would this feel out of place in a **social media** or **SaaS dashboard** screenshot? (It should.)
- [ ] Is there **one clear focal point** — usually a memory, place name, or photo?

## Time & trust

- [ ] Would someone **enjoy revisiting this page a year later** without it feeling dated or embarrassing?
- [ ] Would this still look **good in five years** — no trendy effects?
- [ ] Does every piece of visible content feel **honest and real** — no fake placeholders pretending to be live?

## Content vs chrome

- [ ] Does **content lead** (memory, image, place name) — or does UI chrome lead (buttons, badges, banners)?
- [ ] Are we showing **one primary action** — not a wall of equal buttons?
- [ ] Is metadata **supporting the story**, not replacing it?

## Reflection vs consumption

- [ ] Does this encourage **reflection** (read, remember, record) instead of **consumption** (scroll faster, click more)?
- [ ] If this is a list, does it feel like a **curated shelf** — not a firehose?
- [ ] Would removing engagement metrics make this **better**? If yes, they don’t belong.

## Human tone

- [ ] Is all copy **warm, specific, and non-technical**?
- [ ] Do empty and error states **invite** — not blame or expose systems?
- [ ] Is Korean the **primary language** of the experience?

## Sensory quality

- [ ] Do photos and crops feel **true to place** — not stock or misleading?
- [ ] Is there **enough space** to breathe — would removing one element improve calm?
- [ ] Does motion **confirm** — not entertain?

## Accessibility & inclusion

- [ ] Is text **readable** in bright daylight and at night?
- [ ] Are touch targets **comfortable** on a phone, on location?
- [ ] Does the screen work with **motion reduced**?

---

## Closing Note

Re:Place wins when users forget they are using software — when they feel they are **turning pages in a book they wrote themselves**.

Design System v2 governs consistency.  
**This guide governs soul.**

When the two disagree, ask: *Does this help someone keep a place they loved?*  
If yes, find a calm way to build it. If no, it doesn’t belong — no matter how polished it looks.
